Project Statement

Decoding Singapore's Political Diversity


Singapore, a tiny island nation in Southeast Asia, has defied the odds to emerge as a global economic powerhouse. Central to its remarkable transformation is the steadfast leadership of the PAP, led by the iconic figure of Lee Kuan Yew. Since gaining independence in 1965, the PAP has maintained an unbroken grip on power, shaping policies and governance with a vision of stability, progress, and prosperity.

But what lies beneath this political dominance? What factors have contributed to the PAP's enduring success at the polls? Through interactive data visualizations and insightful analysis, we delve into the complexities of Singaporean politics, questioning, and probing the reasons behind the PAP's resilience.




Overview

This project seeks to address the seemingly paradoxical phenomenon of long-term rule by a single political party in Singapore, despite being a Westminster-styled democracy with free and fair elections. The dominance of the People's Action Party (PAP) in Singapore's political landscape contradicts expectations based on conventional democratic principles.

As Singapore continues to evolve politically, with emerging voices from civil society and opposition parties challenging the PAP's dominance, there is a pressing need to analyze the implications of these changes and identify opportunities for policy reform.

The Task

The Need for the project

Understanding the dynamics of political dominance in Singapore is crucial for assessing the country's democratic health and governance effectiveness. It raises questions about the balance between political stability and diversity, as well as the implications for citizen participation and representation.

The Data

To address the guiding questions posed in the introduction, a variety of data sources will be utilized

1. Historical Election Data: Election results and voting patterns including parliamentary seat distributions and popular vote percentages,
2. Voter Demographics: Demographic data such as age, ethnicity, income level, and education level for voters in Singaporean constituencies.
3. Policy Data: Analysis of key policies, historical context, official statements, speeches, and publications regarding political diversity and governance philosophy.
